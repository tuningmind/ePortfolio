Does My Friend’s Uncle’s Early Death Prove That Exercise Doesn’t Protect against Heart Disease?

Writing prompt:

You ask your friend if she wants to go to the gym with you. “No,” she says, “I never exercise.” You tell her that regular exercise has all kinds of health benefits, including greatly reducing the risk of heart disease.  “I don’t believe that,” she replies, “I had an uncle who got up at 6 am every day of his life to go jogging, and he still died of a heart attack at age 53.” What would you tell your friend?  Does her uncle’s case prove that exercise really doesn’t protect against heart disease after all?

My friend has some confused beliefs about heart attacks and heart disease. Heart disease doesn’t necessarily mean having heart attacks, and heart attacks can sometimes happen even without heart disease. She is also confused about what risk means; her response shows she believes that risk is in one person, so that if you exercise, you should not have heart disease. She also seems to believe that if someone who exercises dies of a heart attack, then exercise did not protect them.

If she believes these things, probably she would not change her mind just because I gave her a list of facts and logical arguments. So the main thing I would tell her is “You should go to the gym with me because you’re my friend and I want your company, and I’m going to the gym now.” But if there were signs that she was willing to consider another perspective, I would say something like the following:

That doesn’t prove that exercise doesn’t protect against heart disease. There are lots of other reasons your uncle could have died from a heart attack even though he exercised every day. Maybe he had one of those rare kinds of heart attacks that are caused by exercise (like Long-QT Syndrome1). Maybe he would have died a lot sooner if he had not been exercising. Maybe he would not have died if he was closer to help. Maybe he had higher risk because of his parents’ heart health, that we don’t know about.

Also, risk isn’t for one person. It’s for a whole population, just like one person doesn’t make a city. If you had a whole city, the city wouldn’t be in the people. Risk of heart disease isn’t in one person either, it’s over a population of people. You can find your own risk of heart disease using the risk score calculator from the Mayo Clinic. If you mark yourself as sedentary, record your score, then do it again and mark yourself active, you can see how much of a difference in risk you have of heart disease in the next 30 years. But this is just a possibility based on what is known from hundreds of thousands of people. When you mark all your risk factors as good in the calculator, there is still some risk that you will come down with heart disease. The risk does not go down to zero. It wouldn’t have gone down to zero for your uncle either.

And I’m sorry he died so young. You must have loved him. Would you like to tell me about him?
