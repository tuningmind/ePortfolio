Reflection

My biggest take-away from the experience of this course is the power of process.

At the beginning of the semester, while I was prepared for the writing involved by the experience I had in Eng 1010, the idea of investigating a social justice issue and presenting an intervention seemed a bit magical. I could imagine some of the writing, but could not imagine what I would wind up proposing or how I would decide what to propose.

Just prior to enrolling in this class I had completed a 12-week professional training. The essence of the training was to become aware of fears that had been flitting quickly by, triggering panic. I learned to slow down and to craft experiences that could teach my flitting brain to stay engaged. In a class where an entire project is assigned, as in “Submit an annotated bibliography of 5 articles,” the student is left to figure out all the process and pieces. In the past, that’s the sort of thing my brain wanted to panic about.

Yet each step of this course took me through incremental steps that completely matched the what I had learned in the training. Every step felt understandable. “Pick a topic you care about,” was the first one. That was not hard to identify, because I had been talking to my friends about polarization. “Write about why you care about this topic,” was the next one. That took a bit more thought, but it was a single topic to reflect on; why do I care about polarization? That single question made it easy to work on the assignment. Fitting the answer into the genre provided, flash memoir, felt like a natural way to write about the answer, and made sense of the writing choices. “Now make a proposal for what to research,” was the next. That seemed nicely open-ended, because I could “just” describe what my expectations were. None of these steps seemed scary, or engaged the panic fairy. Each one had a clear question and purpose and a set of guidelines for making choices.

I remember the first time we did a timed free-writing. The instruction to not cross out and to keep writing seemed challenging at first. My brain desperately wanted me to come up with a final product, and my hand balked. But the other assignments had worked, and I felt like the instruction was sympathetic to me, and I was willing to try venturing into that blank space. There was something very elegant-feeling about having the free-writing be graded only for submission, and not on content. It felt like a bow to my process.

Talking to other students about their drafts was also a sneakily powerful experience, especially for Project 2. Having to get that draft ready for a due date was motivating in a couple of ways. Of course, we always talk about due dates at school, but here, the due date was part of a process, not only with separate grades for that process, but for classmates to read, rather than the teacher. This did something really powerful. It was surprisingly motivating to have something to show my classmates, yet surprisingly gentle to not have to make an “actual” submission. In each case when we were doing peer reviews, I felt unprepared to put ideas to paper, but because of the well-crafted push and pull of the assignment, I felt willing to throw down whatever I had in mind at that point. And the big surprise was, it was just fine. Just like in the other training I’d had prior to this class, once my brain slowed down enough to engage with the process, panic had no basis. I could experience that my writing was good enough.

It would be hard to overstate the power of that kind of recognition.

As I worked on Project 1 and 2, and even for much of the way through Project 3, I had an idea for what I might wind up doing for the Intervention piece. As it turned out, the idea I wound up with at the end was quite different. But it’s clear to me that the effort to do all the intervening steps made it possible for me to relax enough to come up with a good idea. It seemed last minute, but in fact it was engaging with the process all semester that made the final idea possible.

I now feel much less intimidated by the idea of future writing projects, and feel more willing to engage in the smaller steps that make it possible to created polished finished work.
