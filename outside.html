Better Angels
A movement to depolarize America

November 2018

This summer I had the opportunity to observe a 6-hour workshop held by Better Angels. Eight Republicans and eight Democrats sat down to share with each other what it feels like in the current US political climate. They came in tense and on guard, hopeful and worried. As the exercises proceeded, they sat back a little and their faces relaxed. Tension was replaced by curiosity and tentative hope. By the end of the workshop, small groups of Republicans and Democrats were standing around chatting on their own. They expressed some realistic hope that it might be possible for us to work together on both sides of the aisle. No one expressed unguarded optimism, and there was still a sense that there is a lot of work to do, but there was definitely a change in how they felt in each other's presence. This is what I want to work to facilitate.

January 2019

This month I wanted to try my hand at organizing and running a Better Angels-style workshop. I'm not yet a certified moderator, but I wanted to see what it might be like, so I invited two blues and two reds together and ran two of the exercises from the red-blue workshop. One of the participants had a hard time understanding the stereotypes exercise, and found it difficult to respond to the hypothetical "what does the other side think of my side?" This turned out to make it difficult for that person to get the benefit of humanizing the others. It made me wonder whether the workshop design might be unintentionally geared for a higher education level. I'd like to try this again with a less educated group and see how we could work with that.

It was also very difficult for some of the participants to stick with thinking about what the other side believes about itself rather than trying to defend their own position. The conversation guidelines for red-blue workshops are that participants are not there to change each other's minds, so I had to exercise my moderator hat to keep the conversation away from arguing positions. It was challenging and exhilarating, and intervening made the event more meaningful for everyone than if people talked without guidance.

Despite that, though, it was very gratifying to see the way it affected people to hear the other side describe what they care about. Reds described how unsafe they felt in another country and how that affects their views about the wall. Blues who were guided to listen to this reflected out loud that the wall had always seemed mean to them, but now they could consider it from the perspective of safety. I considered this a success moment - there were no opinions changed about the wall but people listened to each other with the intent to understand. Very cool!

February 2019

This month I had an opportunity to attend a red-blue workshop as a participant. This time I got to be on the receiving end of the exercises, and experience what it's like to come up with stereotypes that the other side has of you. For me the most interesting part came during the curiosity questions. This exercise has each side coming up with questions for the other side that are not "gotcha" questions. "How could you believe that...?" is a gotcha question. "What do you believe about...?" is a curiosity question. These turned out to feel quite humbling and, in a way, intimate. The rules did not allow for follow-up questions on this, but the openness it generated left us wanting further conversation.

I should add that this group had some people who are on the farther sides of their respective groups, including a socialist who thinks we are not socialist enough, and an anti-abortion activist. So the experience of feeling warm and curious toward the other side seemed especially noticeable.

March 2019

This month I have organized a follow-up meeting with the reds and blues that were at the February workshop. We'll be meeting again with half reds and half blues. Instead of doing the perspecitive-taking exercises from the red-blue workshop, we'll be seeing what it's like to continue the conversation on specific topics.

One of the things I've noticed about these meetings is that the presence of a moderator makes all the difference in how well the conversations go. It also makes all the difference that the conversation is not open to whatever type of interaction people want, but directed to specific activities that have been shown to increase perspective-taking. Since this next meeting will not include the specific activities, I want to do something else to make sure the conversations are directed. There are several ideas from previous meetings that I want to use. One is to read at the beginning a statement about what rules we will use during the conversation, and ask everyone's permission for me to act as moderator and hold us accountable for using those rules. That would be for things that help keep it civil and on-topic. Next I want to have everyone list topics they would want to cover in this conversation, and then vote, so that we are talking about a topic that is relevant to everyone there. And then I want to review the difference between a "gotcha" question and a curiousity question, and have our questions of each other be vetted with a partner before being actually posed to the other side.

I can't wait!

June 2019

Next steps for me and Better Angels: I finally took the plunge and applied to become a moderatorr. It will take a few weeks to hear back, and they are just in the middle of putting on their yearly convention, so I don't expect to hear back for a couple of weeks yet.

I also met with another member in the area who is interested in getting involved but has a different emphasis. She likes to do the organizing kind of activities that I don't love to do, so it looks like it will be a good pairing. It's great to have a like-minded soul to work with and figure out how to move forward.

So many Americans are concerned about the tone of public discourse in our country - it's a great time to get involved with the depolarization movement!

Books
recently read or much beloved
  • The Blueprint: The Evolutionary Origins of a Good Society by Nikolas Christakis

    Zoom out from human history and look at humans in evolutionary time. Early on, we became social, and from there, any feature that increased survival when we cooperated was selected for. It had to be so, or we would either not have survived, or we would not be social. It's easy to feel grim when focused on smaller timespans, especially since it was also beneficial for survival to pay more attention to potential danger than to what's already working. But Christakis argues that we should remember evolutionary time, because "The arc of history is long, but it bends towards goodness."

    • The Illumination by Kevin Brockmeier

      At 8:17 on a Friday night, wounds begin to glow. Every pain and injury everyone has becomes a light source that everyone can see. Headaches are a soft glow over the head, leukemia a shining light from the bones that looks like holding a flashlight up to your hand, cuts flare out a spear of light. At the same time, a journal consisting of a wife's record of all the love notes her husband has given her every day finds its way into the hands of a series of other people who are touched by it. "I love the soft blue veins on your wrist. I love your lopsided smile. I love watching TV and shelling sunflower seeds with you." Pain and love in a weird but touching story.

    • The Bondwoman's Narrative by Hannah Crafts

      Possibly the first novel written by a black woman slave, I was surprised at how gripping I found the story. The manuscript for this novel turned up a few years ago, and the story of its authentication is also fascinating. The description of how the paper was tested and text examined and so on reminds me of The Weight of Ink (reviewed below) and made both stories more meaningful. The historians figure that the story is mostly autobiographical, so the drama of dealing with different masters and the daring escapes seem all the more real.

    • The Practice Effect by David Brin

      A physicist falls into an alternate world where the laws of physics don't quite work the same. Devices of all kinds can be formed, but the more they are used, the more suited for that use they become. If you leave things around without using them, they forget what they were for. The combination means that objects have to constantly be practiced for their use. A stick of wood with a rough stone used as a hammer becomes increasingly well shaped and held together the more it is used to hammer with; a wall becomes thicker where it is touched. Poor people are employed sitting in chairs so they can become more chair-like, rich people are followed around by servants with the same body type who are wearing clothes that get improved by wearing. A funny and inventive way to think about how things can be different - and the same.

    • The Storytelling Animal: How Stories Make Us Human by Jonathan Gottschall

      We want to be the heroes of our story, and in some sense a therapist is someone who helps you rewrite your story to one you can bear to be in - a kind of story doctor. In the research literature, it's called reappraisal or reframing, but a story doctor is my kind of story.

    • The Five Types of People Who Can Ruin Your Life by Bill Eddy

      There actually are people who can ruin your life. It is not just for tv shows. They have traits that make them recognizable, and Eddy, who started out as a therapist and then became in turn a mediator and lawyer, is expert at picking out the ones who will suck the life out of you. I had assumed it was people who were mentally ill or personality disordered, but lots of them just go about living their lives as best they can, he says, without causing any grief. It's the ones who are willing to hurt you that he warns against, and shares the surprisingly common strategies and traits you can identify them with. I would send this to my younger self, in repeated deliveries, so that if she didn't read the first copy she found, maybe she'd read it if she came across it enough times. Like seatbelts and teaching little kids how to recognize who they shouldn't talk to, this book is something I want everyone I care about to know.

    • When Breath Becomes Air by Paul Kalanithi

      A doctor discovers he has terminal cancer, and chooses to write about the process of coming to terms with death and dying. While reading this, you can't help feeling your own humanity, and pondering how you want to spend your life.

    • The Order of Time by Carlo Rovelli

      A physicist writes about the strange properties of the universe that generate the human experience of time. Strange and fascinating. I many have thought that time dilation was for tiny, super-speedy particles, but turns out we can feel it - it feels like down. The increased gravity closer to the earth that our feet feel is the difference in the passage of time between our head and our feet. I had to walk around wondering at my feet for half a day after reading this.

    • The Philosopher's Flight: A Novel by Tom Miller

      The front cover boasts "Half Science, Half Magic, Entirely Fantastic." The magic and fantastic seem accurate enough, as far as the genre goes, and the science is pretty un-sciency, but what makes this book is the perspective of a boy who wants to grow up to do a woman's work, and isn't allowed because of sexism. Although in the story women have been flying for some decades now, it is believed that men can never be as good at it as women because they are just too big. He has to order special equipment to fit him, and he's subject to cruel pranks at the all-women school he's being allowed to attend. It's a life of a thousand paper cuts, of a boy. Lots of hilariously familiar moments had me savoring the telling of this story, far more than other kinds of stories of its genre.

    • It's All Relative: Adventures Up and Down the World's Family Tree by A.J. Jacobs

      A journalist embeds himself in life to see what it means - in other books, to live every commandment in the Bible, or gain bodily perfection. In this one, he organizes a family reunion for the entire human family.

    • The Wizard and the Prophet: Two Remarkable Scientists and Their Dueling Visions to Shape Tomorrow's World by Charles C. Mann

      One brought us wheat that could grow more than it ever had before. One made us notice that the effects of our actions are affecting the entire ecosystem. Both care about saving and enriching lives. But the values that shaped their work were quite different, and we see the implications playing out around us.

    • The Last Bookaneer: A Novel by Matthew Pearl

      Pearl writes historical fiction set in Boston at the very beginning of MIT. At a time when engineering is viewed with disdain and application of science is considered work for lesser minds, the visionary first president of MIT begins to teach applied science. But naturally some forces resist, and steampunk highjinks ensue. Great fun.

    • The Ego Tunnel: The Science of the Mind and the Myth of the Self by Thomas Metzinger

      You feel like a self because a self comes into creation each time you wake up. But it's a construction of the mind, probably to allow different parts of the mind to communicate with each other in a flexible, real-time way that is highly adaptive to changing circumstance. I'm sure my description is doing an injustice to Metzinger's highly nuanced arguments and cases.

    • The Culture Code: The Secrets of Highly Successful Groups by Daniel Coyle

      The author's previous book, The Talent Code, was a game-changing book for me. This one comes close to matching the impact of the first, with the idea of belonging cues, which affect how groups coalesce.

    • I Thought It Was Just Me (but it isn't): Making the Journey from "What Will People Think?" to "I Am Enough" Brene Brown

      Brene Brown writes openly about feelings of shame we all prefer to hide, and normalizes our feelings in the process. I knew her message was starting to sink in when someone in a math class asked a question I was thinking about asking, and I realized that if I'd asked the question, I would have seemed just as normal as that other person.

    • The Mind Illuminated: A Complete Meditation Guide Integrating Buddhist Wisdom and Brain Science for Greater Mindfulness by John Yates and Matthew Immergut

      Detailed instructions for why and how to notice the actions of your own mind. When the title says "complete," they're not kidding. This is very thorough. Not so much on the brain science, though. There are few science references, although some very general brain-sounding terms are used. The main way the brain science shows up is that the author does not interpret Buddhism in mystical terms; karma is in this life, once-returning is a state the brain can achieve through practice and has to do with attention and joy, not reincarnation. Practicing is not a mystical experience, but a very specific set of brain-training activities.

    • The Weight of Ink by Rachel Kadish

      Since this one does not have ": A Novel" in the title, I have to specify that this is a novel. A brilliant woman who is born just before the Great Plague hits London learns to be a scholar by reading for a rabbi blinded in the Inquisition. His need for her reading gives her the opportunity to use her mind in a way she would never be allowed to otherwise, and she out-philsophizes the most gifted philosophers of her time - under a pen name. Hundreds of years later, an odd-couple of researchers discover themselves other as they discover her. So many quotable lines, I plan to reread this with a pencil.

    • Algorithms to Live By: The Computer Science of Human Decisions by Brian Christian and Tom Griffiths

    • At the Edge of Uncertainty: 11 Discoveries Taking Science by Surprise by Michael Brooks

    • Sum: Forty Tales from the Afterlives by David Eagleman

    • The Antidote: Happiness for People Who Can't Stand Positive Thinking by Oliver Burkeman

    • 21 Lessons for the 21st Century by Yuval Noah Harari
