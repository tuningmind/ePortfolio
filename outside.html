Better Angels
A movement to depolarize America

Recently I had the opportunity to observe a 6-hour workshop held by Better Angels. Eight Republicans and eight Democrats sat down to share with each other what it feels like in the current US political climate. They came in tense and on guard, hopeful and worried. As the exercises proceeded, they sat back a little and their faces relaxed. Tension was replaced by curiosity and tentative hope. By the end of the workshop, small groups of Republicans and Democrats were standing around chatting on their own. They expressed some realistic hope that it might be possible for us to work together on both sides of the aisle. No one expressed unguarded optimism, and there was still a sense that there is a lot of work to do, but there was definitely a change in how they felt in each other's presence. This is what I want to work to facilitate.

Books
read this year
  • The Storytelling Animal: How Stories Make Us Human by Jonathan Gottschall

    We want to be the heroes of our story, and in some sense a therapist is someone who helps you rewrite your story to one you can bear to be in - a kind of story doctor. In the research literature, it's called reappraisal or reframing, but a story doctor is my kind of story.

  • The Five Types of People Who Can Ruin Your Life by Bill Eddy

    There actually are people who can ruin your life. It is not just for tv shows. They have traits that make them recognizable, and Eddy, who started out as a therapist and then became in turn a mediator and lawyer, is expert at picking out the ones who will suck the life out of you. I had assumed it was people who were mentally ill or personality disordered, but lots of them just go about living their lives as best they can, he says, without causing any grief. It's the ones who are willing to hurt you that he warns against, and shares the surprisingly common strategies and traits you can identify them with. I would send this to my younger self, in repeated deliveries, so that if she didn't read the first copy she found, maybe she'd read it if she came across it enough times. Like seatbelts and teaching little kids how to recognize who they shouldn't talk to, this book is something I want everyone I care about to know.

  • When Breath Becomes Air by Paul Kalanithi

    A doctor discovers he has terminal cancer, and chooses to write about the process of coming to terms with death and dying. While reading this, you can't help feeling your own humanity, and pondering how you want to spend your life.

  • The Order of Time by Carlo Rovelli

    A physicist writes about the strange properties of the universe that generate the human experience of time. Strange and fascinating. I many have thought that time dilation was for tiny, super-speedy particles, but turns out we can feel it - it feels like down. The increased gravity closer to the earth that our feet feel is the difference in the passage of time between our head and our feet. I had to walk around wondering at my feet for half a day after reading this.

  • The Philosopher's Flight: A Novel by Tom Miller

    The front cover boasts "Half Science, Half Magic, Entirely Fantastic." The magic and fantastic seem accurate enough, as far as the genre goes, and the science is pretty un-sciency, but what makes this book is the perspective of a boy who wants to grow up to do a woman's work, and isn't allowed because of sexism. Although in the story women have been flying for some decades now, it is believed that men can never be as good at it as women because they are just too big. He has to order special equipment to fit him, and he's subject to cruel pranks at the all-women school he's being allowed to attend. It's a life of a thousand paper cuts, of a boy. Lots of hilariously familiar moments had me savoring the telling of this story, far more than other kinds of stories of its genre.

  • It's All Relative: Adventures Up and Down the World's Family Tree by A.J. Jacobs

    A journalist embeds himself in life to see what it means - in other books, to live every commandment in the Bible, or gain bodily perfection. In this one, he organizes a family reunion for the entire human family.

  • The Wizard and the Prophet: Two Remarkable Scientists and Their Dueling Visions to Shape Tomorrow's World by Charles C. Mann

    One brought us wheat that could grow more than it ever had before. One made us notice that the effects of our actions are affecting the entire ecosystem. Both care about saving and enriching lives. But the values that shaped their work were quite different, and we see the implications playing out around us.

  • The Last Bookaneer: A Novel by Matthew Pearl

    Pearl writes historical fiction set in Boston at the very beginning of MIT. At a time when engineering is viewed with disdain and application of science is considered work for lesser minds, the visionary first president of MIT begins to teach applied science. But naturally some forces resist, and steampunk highjinks ensue. Great fun.

  • The Ego Tunnel: The Science of the Mind and the Myth of the Self by Thomas Metzinger

    You feel like a self because a self comes into creation each time you wake up. But it's a construction of the mind, probably to allow different parts of the mind to communicate with each other in a flexible, real-time way that is highly adaptive to changing circumstance. I'm sure my description is doing an injustice to Metzinger's highly nuanced arguments and cases.

  • The Culture Code: The Secrets of Highly Successful Groups by Daniel Coyle

    The author's previous book, The Talent Code, was a game-changing book for me. This one comes close to matching the impact of the first, with the idea of belonging cues, which affect how groups coalesce.

  • I Thought It Was Just Me (but it isn't): Making the Journey from "What Will People Think?" to "I Am Enough" Brene Brown

    Brene Brown writes openly about feelings of shame we all prefer to hide, and normalizes our feelings in the process. I knew her message was starting to sink in when someone in a math class asked a question I was thinking about asking, and I realized that if I'd asked the question, I would have seemed just as normal as that other person.

  • The Mind Illuminated: A Complete Meditation Guide Integrating Buddhist Wisdom and Brain Science for Greater Mindfulness by John Yates and Matthew Immergut

    Detailed instructions for why and how to notice the actions of your own mind. When the title says "complete," they're not kidding. This is very thorough. Not so much on the brain science, though. There are few science references, although some very general brain-sounding terms are used. The main way the brain science shows up is that the author does not interpret Buddhism in mystical terms; karma is in this life, once-returning is a state the brain can achieve through practice and has to do with attention and joy, not reincarnation. Practicing is not a mystical experience, but a very specific set of brain-training activities.

  • The Weight of Ink by Rachel Kadish

    Since this one does not have ": A Novel" in the title, I have to specify that this is a novel. A brilliant woman who is born just before the Great Plague hits London learns to be a scholar by reading for a rabbi blinded in the Inquisition. His need for her reading gives her the opportunity to use her mind in a way she would never be allowed to otherwise, and she out-philsophizes the most gifted philosophers of her time - under a pen name. Hundreds of years later, an odd-couple of researchers discover themselves other as they discover her. So many quotable lines, I plan to reread this with a pencil.

  • The Craving Mind: From Cigarettes to Smartphones to Love - Why We Get Hooked and How We Can Break Bad Habits by Judson Brewer and Jon Kabat-Zinn

  • Algorithms to Live By: The Computer Science of Human Decisions by Brian Christian and Tom Griffiths

  • At the Edge of Uncertainty: 11 Discoveries Taking Science by Surprise by Michael Brooks

  • Sum: Forty Tales from the Afterlives by David Eagleman

  • The Antidote: Happiness for People Who Can't Stand Positive Thinking Oliver Burkeman
