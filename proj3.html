Writing Prompt

Overview

So far this semester, we have devoted an entire project to the process of invention in order to carefully consider a social issue from multiple perspectives, and an entire project to the process of research in order to gain a fuller understanding of the conversations happening around that social issue. For our final project, we’ll be putting the understanding that you’ve gained through these projects into practice. You will choose one of the disagreements that you discovered in Project 2 and enter into the conversation. Choosing an appropriate audience, you will craft a rhetorical intervention on the social issue.

Written Elements:

This project will have two parts: (1) a multimodal (including both textual and visual elements) composition designed to make an argument about your social issue to a specific audience, and (2) an essay describing the rhetorical choices that you made in designing and creating your intervention.

Rhetorical Intervention Evaluation Criteria:

Physical Requirements: The formatting for this element of the project will depend entirely upon the genre that you choose to work with. The only physical requirement for this element of the project is that you must include both textual and visual elements of some kind.

Your Rhetorical Situation: For this portion of the project, you must choose an audience to focus upon for your persuasion. The argument that you make, the audience that you choose, and the genre that you work with must all be both appropriate for your social issue and must also all work well together.

Content: As with the other elements of this part of the project, the content will largely depend upon your choices of audience and genre; however, the goal of the content must be to persuade your intended audience of a particular idea related to your social issue. This idea could be to take up a position that they don’t have already, to consider a point of view that they may not have been exposed to already or that they may have discounted, to act in specific ways upon a position that they already hold, or any other goal appropriate to your audience and social issue. Regardless of what you choose as your rhetorical goal for this part of the project, the argument must be clear, well-supported, and appropriate for the audience and genre.

Structure and Cohesion: As with the physical requirements, the structure of this portion of the project will be based upon the genre that you choose to work with. That being said, the general principles of coherence and cohesion still apply to any form of writing that you may do. The focus should be clear and consistent throughout the document, and the individual pieces of the composition should be clearly connected for a single, cohesive document. We’ll be talking about visual design principles during this project to aid you in creating coherence and cohesion in visual texts.

Source Usage: No research is required for this project; however, most arguments that you may make about social issues will likely require some kind of expert support. Additionally, while all sources do need to be acknowledged, most public genres typically don’t make use of an academic citation style like MLA or APA. You’ll want to look at genre-specific conventions for referencing sources for whichever genre you choose.

Mechanics and Style: As with every project in this class, a certain degree of conformity to the dialect of Edited American English is expected. That being said, depending up on your genre, some adaptations or exceptions to strict Edited American English may be appropriate. You’ll need to make these decisions as you research the conventions of the genre that you choose to work with. You should also consider how your writing style should shift for your audience as well, particularly in the areas of tone and formality.

Explanation Evaluation Criteria:

Physical Requirements: 600 words minimum. This paper must be typed, double-spaced, in 12 point Times New Roman font, and must have 1 inch top and bottom margins and 1 inch left and right margins. It should also use either MLA or APA formatting.

Your Rhetorical Situation: For this report, you are writing a formal, professional report explaining the rhetorical choices that you made in the other part of this project. Much like the second project, your audience is both your classmates and your instructor. Make sure that your language and tone are appropriate to this situation. You may assume that your audience is familiar with your rhetorical intervention but will not have it in front of them.

Content: This report should provide your reader with detailed information about the choices that you made in designing and constructing the rhetorical intervention portion of this project. This will, at minimum, include the audience, purpose, context, and genre that you’ve worked with. Other things that you may explain include which visual elements you’ve included, the research used to support your argument, the tone/formality/vocabulary, etc.

Structure and Cohesion: While there are some differences between this assignment and Project 2, a professional report and an academic essay have a lot in common when it comes to organization. Effective reports must have a central thought that clearly and consistently guides the direction of the paper. Begin and conclude your review effectively through framing paragraphs that help the reader to understand the overall organization and content of the essay. Your supporting ideas should be organized in a logical manner, progressing from point to point in a way that is easy for your reader to follow. Additionally, be sure that the individual parts of your report are connected effectively and flow together through the use of effective cohesive language that helps your reader to see the connections between your ideas.

Source Usage: No research is required for this part of the project; however, you do need to adequately reference the elements of your rhetorical intervention in order to demonstrate the choices that you’ve made.

Mechanics and Style: As with every project in this class, a certain degree of conformity to the dialect of Edited American English is expected. You are also expected to use a style that is clear, engaging, and appropriate for your rhetorical situation.

Project 3

A Shout-Out for Humans: A Graphic Appeal to Similarity

The topic of my research is depolarization of American voters, and from my research I’ve identified that a good target for intervention is humanization of the other side. While there are many strategies that can foster humanization, one I’m focusing on here is to raise the idea of both sides being human using a bumper sticker or t-shirt design. By using a medium that is easy to access and spread, it could in principle be easy to reach a large number of people whose political alignment is not just my own social group or neighborhood. Anyone driving near the bumper sticker could see it, and anyone walking near someone wearing the t-shirt could see it. Social media could also reach a lot of people, but as Sia et.al. (2002) found, an electronic medium between those posting and viewing the message has a dehumanizing effect. With a bumper sticker or t-shirt, there is an awareness that this particular person put the sticker on the car I’m seeing, or wore the t-shirt I’m seeing. This connection could contribute to the sense of a real person behind the message. In the design itself, my idea is to use the letters of “Republican” and “Democrat” to spell the word “Human,” echoing our common connection.

My overall goal with the design is to have the word “Human” easily readable at first glance, and at second glance, the names of the parties. To accomplish this, I have placed the word “Republican” above the word “Democrat,” with the letters u, m, a, and n much larger than the rest of the lower-case letters. In addition, the letters u, a, and n that have been pulled from the word “Republican” are moved below their baseline, and the “m” from “Democrat” is moved above its baseline, in order to evoke the idea of reaching across a gap or aisle, yet still having them be part of both the party names and the word “Human.”

Overall, there are two considerations for combining the words. One is the appearance of the lower-case letters of the party names. A number of fonts could be suitable, with the requirement that they must be easy to recognize quickly. A san-serif font allows the space to be used all on the essential letter shapes without decoration. I am currently using Avenir Next for its roundness and uncluttered use of space. Whatever size makes the lower-case letters make maximum use of the width of the sticker or t-shirt, I will call 1em, in keeping with a convention from making websites. 1em can be thought of as a unit size, and could represent, say, an 80pt font in this paper, or a 160pt font on a bumper sticker. The actual size of the unit would be determined when working with the printing company. The colors of the 1em-sized letters would be red for the letters from Republican, and blue for the letters from Democrat, since those colors already well established in the culture. The letters u, m, a and n will be black, bold, moved towards the line between the words, and 2em, twice the size of the other letters, so that they will be read as part of the super-imposed word “Human.”

The other consideration for combining the words, how to make the H appear, arises because the H in “Human” is not present in either word. I have chosen to simply place an H at the beginning of the letters, with the same characteristics as the rest of the letters in “Human.” Placing the H at the beginning, however, has the shortcoming of stranding the H by itself in a way that has no obvious meaning. Could a feature like that make the H seem forced, and by extension, subtly suggest that “the other side” can only be thought of as human by some forced and meaningless method?

It seems worthwhile to see if there is a way to combine the R and D artistically to create an H. The shape of the H could be such that it appears the same font and color as the letters u, m, a, and n that have been pulled out of the party names. One spacing issue that arises immediately on attempting this second H-placement is that the letters of the two party names would have to be spaced differently in order to make sense, since the D has to be close enough to the R to be able to be combined. I would then want to move the rest of the letters in “Democrat” so they are spaced the same distance apart. I decided against this because the difference between the two party names seems greater when their spacing is different. Meanwhile, combining the R and D requires some artistry and suitable software. What I would like to do is use a more sophisticated painting program to make both R and D distinct and recognizable without effort, and show the H growing from them. I would also like to ask for advice from art and typography experts to explore this option.

Although the idea of a bumper sticker seemed very promising to me at first, and people have been enthusiastic about the design, this overlooks an important consideration. MacDonald et.al. (2017) point out that reminders of group similarity can threaten social identity. In light of this, a bumper sticker reminding Democrats and Republicans that we are both human could inadvertently strengthen partisanship. For this reason, a t-shirt design might have more potential than a bumper sticker, because it has the potential to facilitate a face to face conversation. In addition, I will add a website along the bottom, either Better Angels or Living Room Conversations, which offers an implied invitation. This could be a part of a larger strategy to help people make opportunities to talk to others with different views and to look for ways to find emotional similarity. Overall, the t-shirt design seems like a good contribution to a larger campaign to create ways to humanize others and seek similarity across the political divide.
